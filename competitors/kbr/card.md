## Overview

KBR, Inc. (Houston, TX) is a roughly $7.8B, ~38,000-person global government-and-industrial-services company running two segments: Mission Technology Solutions (MTS, the former Government Solutions business, about $5.58B in FY2025) and Sustainable Technology Solutions (process technology, energy, and chemicals) [S1][S2][S3]. Its national-security and space franchise was assembled by acquisition: Wyle in 2016 ($600M, test and evaluation of aircraft and advanced systems, systems and sustainment engineering, life sciences, and NASA support dating to Project Gemini), Stinger Ghaffarian Technologies (SGT) in 2018 ($355M, NASA mission operations), Centauri in 2020 (about $800M, military space, directed energy, and cyber, bought from Arlington Capital Partners), Frazer-Nash in 2021 (UK and Australia high-end systems engineering), and LinQuest in 2024 ($737M, Space Force, Air Force, and intelligence space and battlespace integration) [S14][S15][S13][S16][S12]. We collide with KBR at three of our anchor customers. At Redstone, KBR Wyle Services LLC holds Army aviation-and-missile systems-engineering and SETA work for PEO Missiles and Space, including tactical missiles, Patriot, and a multi-weapon-system SETA task order running to 2031 [S11]. At NASA it is the dominant human-spaceflight services incumbent, holding IMOC III (up to $1.9B to plan, train, and fly across JSC, MSFC, and KSC) and the astronaut-health HHPC (estimated $2.459B) [S8][S9]. Across defense T&E, SETA, and military space it competes on the same OASIS+, SeaPort-NxG, and ASTRO vehicles we chase [S17]. Two facts reshape the KBR threat right now: in September 2025 KBR announced it will spin its entire MTS government business into a separate public company (targeted for early 2027), and in its NASA crown jewel it just lost the $1.8B COSMOS recompete and two subsequent protests [S4][S6]. Where we win is not scale. It is focus, dedicated senior attention, and stability against a competitor that is carving out its government arm while its incumbency proves fallible even at home.

## Threat Summary

KBR hurts us most as the entrenched, low-risk incumbent in NASA human spaceflight and as a resident engineering house at Redstone. On the NASA side it runs mission planning, astronaut and flight-controller training, and real-time flight execution for the ISS, Orion, SLS, and Artemis under IMOC III, and it just won the estimated $2.459B astronaut-health HHPC, so on any human-spaceflight-adjacent services bid at JSC or MSFC it carries directly relevant, current past performance and an embedded workforce [S8][S9]. At Redstone, KBR Wyle Services sits inside PEO Missiles and Space doing tactical-missile and Patriot systems engineering and a multi-weapon-system SETA scope through 2031, the same aviation-and-missile customer community we support [S11]. It reaches military-space and connected-battlespace work through LinQuest and Centauri, and it pre-qualifies on most large task orders through ASTRO (eight prime seats), OASIS+, and SeaPort-NxG [S12][S13][S17]. Where KBR seldom beats us is a focused, labor-driven T&E, SETA, or mission-engineering competition scored on dedicated key personnel and stable, senior program attention. And KBR is unusually beatable on continuity right now: it is spinning its entire government business into a new company through early 2027, it lost the $1.8B COSMOS NASA recompete it had effectively held since 2018 and failed two protests, and its government-segment revenue was essentially flat year over year while contingency work in Europe fell away [S4][S5][S6][S2][S1]. Those give us clean, scoreable discriminators on focus, stability, and transition risk.

## Their Discriminators

- NASA human-spaceflight incumbency and workforce. Decades of plan-train-fly and astronaut-health delivery mean the customer sees KBR as the people who train the astronauts and run the mission-control floor, not a vendor to be onboarded [S8][S9][S14].
- Redstone and Huntsville engineering mass through KBR Wyle Services. Resident missile and aviation systems-engineering and SETA staff embedded with PEO Missiles and Space and the AMCOM community [S11].
- Military-space and battlespace breadth from LinQuest and Centauri. Space Force, Air Force, and intelligence relationships, plus directed-energy and cyber, that KBR pulls into adjacent bids [S12][S13].
- Turnkey mission-operations delivery. A single prime bundling engineering, operations, training, and logistics, attractive to a customer who wants one accountable prime for an end-to-end mission [S8].
- Scale, vehicle access, and balance sheet. A ~$7.8B company with roughly $23B in backlog and options and prime seats on ASTRO, OASIS+, and SeaPort-NxG that let it carry big IDIQs at low perceived risk [S1][S2][S17].

## Weaknesses

### W1 - The entire government business is being carved out through early 2027

In September 2025 KBR's board approved a plan to spin off its Mission Technology Solutions segment, the whole government-and-space business that competes with us, into a separate publicly traded company; the spin-off is targeted to complete around January 2027, and as of mid-2026 the new company still had no announced name, a CEO-designate (Michael LaRouche) who does not join until September 2026, and a CFO-designate joining in July 2026 [S4][S5]. For roughly eighteen months the organization we bid against is preoccupied with separating systems, standing up a new public company, retention, and rebranding rather than with any single program. A corporate separation of this scale is exactly the kind of distraction and transition risk a capture team can make scoreable, because it is legitimate to ask how a program will be insulated from the offeror's own reorganization.

### W2 - Incumbency is not holding, even in the NASA crown jewel

KBR lost the $1.8B COSMOS recompete for NASA human-spaceflight mission operations at Johnson Space Center to the Ascend Aerospace and Technology joint venture; COSMOS replaced the Mission Systems Operations work KBR had effectively held since its 2018 acquisition of SGT, and after NASA took corrective action and re-awarded to the same team, GAO denied KBR's follow-on protest in early 2026 [S6][S15]. When a company loses, protests, and loses again in the single franchise where its incumbency is deepest, its safe-incumbent narrative becomes a live question everywhere. Continuity and low transition risk are scoreable criteria a churned incumbent cannot simply assert.

### W3 - Flat government growth and contingency-revenue volatility

KBR's government (MTS) segment revenue was essentially flat in FY2025, rising just $26M to about $5.581B from $5.555B, and total-company fourth-quarter revenue fell 11% partly on reductions in EUCOM contingency scope [S1][S2]. KBR remains active in contingency logistics (it won a LOGCAP V task order over an Amentum Parsons team whose protest GAO dismissed), but that revenue is inherently lumpy and swings the top line quarter to quarter [S7]. A competitor whose core services base is flat, and whose headline movement leans on overseas contingency work rather than steady engineering-services growth in our lanes, is one whose promised reach-back and investment on any single program deserve scrutiny.

### W4 - Multinational prime overhead and wrap on labor-driven competitions

KBR is a ~$7.8B, 38,000-person prime operating in more than 29 countries and carrying corporate overhead, indirect structure, and shareholder-margin expectations (FY2025 adjusted EBITDA margin about 12.4%), now layered with the cost and complexity of a corporate separation [S1][S4]. On T&E, SETA, and mission-engineering competitions where evaluated price turns on the fully burdened labor rate rather than on scale, a large multinational's wrap is beatable by a focused mid-size firm. This is team judgment rather than documented rate intelligence, but it is a repeatable pattern where the buy is dominated by direct labor. ### W5 - Government mission diluted inside an industrial conglomerate

Until the spin-off completes, KBR's defense, space, and NASA work sits inside the same company that licenses more than 85 process technologies and builds ammonia, chemical, and clean-refining plants through its Sustainable Technology Solutions segment [S4]. On a focused test-enterprise, mission-engineering, or Redstone recompete, a dedicated engineering company can credibly argue that the customer's mission is one line inside a diversified industrial portfolio, while for us it is the core of the business. This weakness is time-boxed: once the standalone national-security company launches, KBR's government arm becomes a more focused ~$5B pure-play, so exploit the dilution argument hardest before the separation closes [S4][S5].

## Our Counter-Positioning

## Objection Handling

- **They say:** "KBR trains the astronauts and flies the missions, so nobody knows NASA human spaceflight better." **We say:** Deep familiarity is real, but NASA just moved its $1.8B mission-operations work to a different team and the decision was upheld on protest. Score dedicated key personnel, transition risk, and continuity for this actual scope, not incumbency on programs that are themselves changing hands.
- **They say:** "KBR is a global company with the scale to carry this at low risk." **We say:** That global company is in the middle of splitting its government business into a brand-new public company. Ask every offeror how this program is insulated from their own reorganization, and what percentage of named key personnel are committed full time to it.
- **They say:** "They already sit at Redstone with the missile and aviation engineers." **We say:** Resident staff is a starting point, not a delivery plan. Score the burdened rate, the named key personnel, and the dedicated senior attention this effort will receive, rather than corporate presence spread across missiles, chemicals, and contingency logistics.
- **They say:** "A large incumbent is the safe choice." **We say:** Ask what has changed on their side. A full corporate spin-off, new leadership, flat government revenue, and a recent lost NASA recompete make continuity the real risk question. A stable, focused team is the lower-risk path on a labor-driven scope.

## Intel & Proof Points

### PP1 - Scale and FY2025 financials

KBR reported FY2025 (fiscal year ended January 2, 2026) revenue of about $7.79B (up 1%), net income attributable to KBR of $415M (up 11%), and adjusted EBITDA of $968M (a 12.4% margin, up 12%), with diluted EPS of $3.21 and adjusted EPS of $3.93; it guided FY2026 to revenue of $7.90B-$8.36B and adjusted EBITDA of $980M-$1,040M, and employs about 38,000 people across more than 29 countries [S1][S2].

### PP2 - Government (MTS) segment and backlog

KBR's Mission Technology Solutions segment (the former Government Solutions business, renamed in a January 2025 realignment) generated about $5.581B of FY2025 revenue with $463M of operating income (up 12% from $415M in FY2024); total-company backlog and options were roughly $23.2B (about $16.9B firm), up 13% [S1][S2][S3].

### PP3 - Planned spin-off of the government business

On September 24, 2025 KBR's board approved a plan to spin off Mission Technology Solutions into a separate, publicly traded U.S. company (intended to be tax-free); the spun-off national-security-and-space company is described as entering the market with more than $5B in annual revenue and about 20,000 employees, while the remaining New KBR keeps the process-technology and sustainability business under CEO Stuart Bradie [S4].

### PP4 - Spin-off leadership and timeline

On June 25, 2026 KBR named Michael LaRouche President and CEO-designate of the spin-off company (joining September 24, 2026) and Nicholas Veasey CFO-designate (joining July 1, 2026); the new company's name and brand were still to be announced as of mid-2026, with the separation targeted for completion around January 4, 2027 [S5].

### PP5 - Lost the COSMOS NASA recompete and two protests

NASA awarded the $1.8B COSMOS contract (Contract for Organizing Spaceflight Mission Operations and Systems, a five-year base plus options for Johnson Space Center human-spaceflight mission operations, systems engineering, R&D, IT, and technical services) to the Ascend Aerospace and Technology joint venture; the award replaced the Mission Systems Operations work KBR had held since its 2018 SGT acquisition. KBR protested, NASA took corrective action and re-awarded to the same team, and GAO denied KBR's follow-on protest in early 2026 [S6][S15].

### PP6 - IMOC III human-spaceflight contract

In 2023 NASA awarded KBR IMOC III, worth up to $1.9B over a five-year base plus two two-year options, to provide mission planning, astronaut and Mission Control Center flight-controller training, real-time flight execution, and exploration-vehicle design across JSC, Marshall Space Flight Center, and Kennedy Space Center, supporting the ISS, Commercial Crew, Orion, SLS, Gateway, Human Landing System, and Artemis [S8].

### PP7 - Flat government growth and contingency-revenue volatility

KBR's Mission Technology Solutions revenue rose only $26M year over year in FY2025 (to about $5.581B from $5.555B), and total-company fourth-quarter revenue fell 11% partly on reductions in EUCOM contingency scope, underscoring how much of KBR's near-term revenue movement comes from lumpy overseas contingency logistics rather than steady growth in its core engineering-services lanes; KBR remains an active contingency-logistics prime, having won a LOGCAP V task order over an Amentum Parsons team whose protest GAO dismissed [S1][S2][S7].

### PP8 - NASA astronaut-health (HHPC) contract

In August 2025 NASA awarded KBR the follow-on Human Health and Performance Contract, an estimated $2.459B single-award IDIQ (total value with options about $3.6B) beginning a five-year period of performance on November 1, 2025 with two option periods potentially extending to 2035, largely at Johnson Space Center, covering astronaut health, occupational health, and human-spaceflight health research [S9].

### PP9 - Redstone Army missile engineering and SETA

KBR Wyle Services LLC performs Army systems-engineering and technical-assistance work at Redstone Arsenal, including a cost-plus-fixed-fee SETA task order for multiple air- and ground-launched weapon systems (digital transformation, program management, engineering, software, cybersecurity, and logistics) with a cumulative face value near $207.9M and completion in December 2031; earlier Redstone awards include a $165M tactical-missile engineering task order for the PEO Missiles and Space TAGM Project Office (HELLFIRE and JAGM) and about $207M of Patriot missile systems-engineering work [S11].

### PP10 - LinQuest acquisition

KBR acquired LinQuest Corporation for $737M net (a $745M base price), closing in September 2024; LinQuest brought about 1,550 employees and space, air-dominance, and connected-battlespace engineering and integration for Space Force, Air Force, and intelligence customers, including AI and machine-learning work [S12].

### PP11 - Centauri acquisition

KBR acquired Centauri, LLC for about $800M in cash (net of tax benefits) in October 2020, from Arlington Capital Partners, expanding into military space, directed energy, cyber, and other advanced national-security technology [S13].

### PP12 - Wyle acquisition and T&E heritage

KBR acquired Wyle for $600M in July 2016, adding test and evaluation of aircraft, advanced systems, and networks, systems and sustainment engineering, program and acquisition management, life-science research, and space-medical operations; Wyle had supported NASA since Project Gemini in the 1960s [S14].

### PP13 - SGT acquisition

KBR acquired Stinger Ghaffarian Technologies (SGT) for a $355M base price in April 2018, adding NASA mission operations, engineering, scientific, and IT services; this is the origin of the human-spaceflight mission-operations work later recompeted as COSMOS [S15].

### PP14 - Frazer-Nash acquisition

KBR acquired UK and Australia systems-engineering and advisory firm Frazer-Nash Consultancy from Babcock International for about £293M in October 2021, adding high-end systems engineering, systems assurance, and advisory across defense, energy, and critical infrastructure [S16].

### PP15 - Contract-vehicle access

KBR holds eight prime seats on GSA ASTRO (services for manned, unmanned, and optionally manned platforms and robotics, administered by GSA FEDSIM), and KBR Wyle Services holds OASIS+ and SeaPort-NxG positions, pre-qualifying it on most large services task-order competitions [S17].

### PP16 - Marshall Space Flight Center spaceflight operations (heritage)

KBR (as KBRwyle) previously won a $570.3M NASA contract to develop and execute spaceflight operations at Marshall Space Flight Center in Huntsville, placing it directly in our MSFC anchor customer; this award dates to about 2019 and its current status should be verified before use in a specific pursuit [S10].

### PP17 - Leadership and executive changes

Stuart Bradie is KBR's President and CEO (and chair-elect, slated to lead the remaining New KBR); Byron Bright, formerly President of Government Solutions U.S., became Chief Operating Officer in May 2025; and Shad Evans succeeded Mark Sopp as CFO effective January 5, 2026 [S3][S18].
