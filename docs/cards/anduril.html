Overview

Anduril is a venture-backed defense product company founded in 2017, headquartered in Costa Mesa, CA, that builds autonomous hardware (counter-UAS towers and interceptors, autonomous aircraft and undersea vehicles, rocket motors) tied together by its Lattice command-and-control software [S26]. It self-funds development with private capital and sells finished products at firm-fixed prices instead of performing cost-type development work [S28]. It reported roughly $2.2B revenue in 2025, projects $4.3B for 2026, employs about 7,000 people, and raised a $5B Series H in May 2026 at a $61B valuation [S1][S2][S30]. It grows by acquisition (Area-I 2021, Dive Technologies 2022, Adranos 2023, Blue Force Technologies 2023, Numerica's radar/C2 lines 2025) [S26][S27]. We do not usually meet them head-to-head on a services RFP. The collision is structural: their enterprise agreements and "systems integration partner" roles absorb integration, test-support, fielding, and sustainment-engineering scope that agencies used to buy from services firms like us, at SOCOM (C-UAS and mission autonomy SIP) [S7][S9], the Army (a $20B, 10-year enterprise agreement) [S3][S4], and the USAF test enterprise, where their CCA is now flown and turned by blue-suit crews at Edwards under the Eglin-based Experimental Operations Unit [S12][S13]. They are also building a Huntsville presence aimed at the missile, C-UAS, and air-and-missile-defense community on our home turf [S16].

Threat Summary

Anduril rarely beats us in a source selection; it removes the source selection. When a customer signs an enterprise agreement or names them integration partner, the integration labs, test support, fielding, and software-sustainment tasks we would have bid become vendor-delivered product scope [S3][S4][S7]. Watch three lanes: (1) Army/Redstone, where the $20B enterprise agreement consolidates 120+ procurement actions and their Huntsville office (led by retired LTG Neil Thurgood) targets C-UAS and air-and-missile-defense work [S4][S16]; (2) SOCOM, where SIP roles for counter-UAS and mission autonomy put them in the integrator seat we sell into [S7][S9]; (3) the USAF test enterprise, where their CCA test narrative ("maintainers run it from a ruggedized laptop") argues for smaller government test and ops-support footprints [S12]. They also outbid everyone for autonomy and software talent, including in Huntsville [S16].

Their Discriminators

  • Speed and willingness to build first: hardware developed on their own money and offered as a working product, not a proposal, which buys credibility with impatient customers [S28].
  • Lattice software as the connective layer: once it is the C2 backbone (JIATF-401 named it the preferred tactical counter-drone C2) every added sensor or effector deepens lock-in [S6].
  • Capital depth: $5B Series H and roughly $11.9B raised lets them absorb losses that would kill a services firm's balance sheet [S1][S30].
  • Appetite for firm-fixed-price risk at scales primes now avoid; all task orders under the Army enterprise agreement are FFP [S5].
  • Political and narrative top-cover as the flagship "new defense industrial base" company; Golden Dome software role with Palantir extends that story [S22].
  • Senior-leader hires who open doors: retired LTG Neil Thurgood (former Army RCCTO) runs their Huntsville site and portfolio [S16].

Strengths

  • Marquee enterprise positions: Army 10-year, up-to-$20B enterprise agreement (Mar 2026) consolidating 120+ procurement actions [S3][S4]; SOCOM counter-UAS SIP IDIQ (~$967.6M, Jan 2022; ceiling raised to ~$1.9B in Nov 2024) [S7][S8]; SOCOM mission autonomy SIP ($86M, Mar 2025) [S9].
  • Production wins in USAF autonomy: YFQ-44A CCA first flew Oct 2025, entered serial production Mar 2026, and was ordered into production alongside GA's FQ-42 in Jun 2026 (150 combined aircraft planned by 2030) [S10][S11].
  • Embedded in the USAF test enterprise: the 53rd Wing's Experimental Operations Unit flew multi-sortie CCA tests at Edwards in Apr 2026 with front-line maintainers launching and recovering the jet [S12][S13].
  • Soldier program footholds: took over Microsoft's IVAS contract by novation (Apr 2025) and won a $159M SBMC prototype award (Sep 2025), teamed with Meta on the EagleEye headset [S14][S15][S29].
  • Large fielded base at DHS/CBP: autonomous surveillance towers under an IDIQ with a $2B ceiling, ~$818M obligated as of Dec 2025 [S21].
  • International proof: first Ghost Shark XL-AUV delivered to the Royal Australian Navy (Nov 2025) under an A$1.7B production program [S23].
  • Financial runway and growth: $2.2B (2025) to projected $4.3B (2026) revenue, $61B valuation [S1][S2].
  • Growing weapons/propulsion play: Adranos acquisition (2023) and a Mississippi solid-rocket-motor complex aimed at the SRM shortage [S18][S26].

Weaknesses

W1 — Execution strain and quality escapes under hypergrowth

A March 2026 WIRED investigation, based on interviews with current and former employees, described safety lapses, production delays, management churn, and deadline pressure across manufacturing sites; it reported the SRM line missed its July 2025 mass-production target by more than a year, that the head of production was let go abruptly, and that in at least four military tests in 2025 Anduril systems (including two Altius drones) failed to perform as expected [S18]. In late June 2026 a rocket motor exploded during a test at their Mississippi site, damaging the test stand (no injuries reported) [S19].

W2 — Deep operating losses carried by venture capital

The company projects an operating loss of roughly $1.2B for 2026 while funding simultaneous factory build-outs, with adjusted EBITDA profitability not expected until about 2030 [S1]. Every task order under the Army enterprise agreement is firm-fixed-price, so overruns land on their books, a structure that has burned far larger primes [S5]. Long-horizon support commitments depend on continued access to private capital markets.

W3 — Integrator roles that conflict with their product line

As SOCOM's counter-UAS SIP they are chartered to integrate "best-of-breed" third-party sensors and effectors while simultaneously selling their own competing towers, interceptors, and C2 software [S7][S8]. On IVAS they took over sustainment of another vendor's hardware while positioning their own EagleEye headset for the follow-on SBMC competition [S14][S15]. Our read: customers and competitors will increasingly raise organizational-conflict-of-interest and vendor-lock objections against vendor-owned integration.

W4 — Uneven reliability record on fielded, long-duration systems

EFF reporting on CBP's tower program, citing 2024 Border Patrol memos, said about 30% of the towers were broken and that the sentry towers have persistent performance problems in desert conditions [S20]. The fielded-fleet availability story is far weaker than the demo story.

W5 — Consolidation model draws scrutiny, and they litigate

Press coverage of the $20B Army enterprise agreement flags concentration and fixed-price risk as open questions for a deal of this size [S5]. They are also willing to fight procurements: Anduril protested an Air Force ABMS tactical-edge-node task order at GAO (B-419420); the protest was denied and partly dismissed [S17]. Judgment: single-vendor enterprise deals are politically fragile and give incumbents and Congress a target.

Our Counter-Positioning

  • Sell independence. They cannot objectively grade their own homework (W3). Position Astrion as the vendor-neutral engineering and T&E arm that makes the government a smart buyer of exactly this kind of product: independent test planning, acceptance criteria, integration verification, and fleet-availability measurement. Their growth makes our referee role more necessary, not less.
  • Own the government side of the table. On CCA, C-UAS, and enterprise-software fielding, somebody must define requirements, run government test, and hold FFP vendors accountable for W1/W4-type escapes. That is SETA, T&E, and mission-engineering scope only a non-conflicted firm can hold.
  • Exploit the sustainment gap. Their DNA is product launch, not decades of on-range, on-site support (W1, W4). Emphasize our staffed, badged, enduring presence at AFTC, Redstone, and customer sites, and our record of keeping government test infrastructure running.
  • Make availability data the battleground. Push customers to demand fielded-system readiness metrics and government-verified test results before enterprise commitments (W4). We can build and run those scorecards.
  • Team where it pays. Relationship may become "both": their products need range time, instrumentation, test conduct, safety packages, and V&V we already deliver inside the fence. Pursue sub/associate roles on their test campaigns rather than ceding the whole lane. (needs internal input: any existing teaming contacts or NDAs with them)
  • Defend the talent base. Expect Huntsville and Edwards/Eglin poaching at product-company compensation (per their Huntsville expansion [S16]). (needs internal input: attrition data and counter-offer posture for autonomy/software staff)

Objection Handling

  • They say: "They deliver working hardware on their own dime; you deliver briefings and labor hours." We say: "Self-funded hardware still has to pass somebody's independent test, integrate with everything the vendor doesn't sell, and stay mission-capable for years. That verification and integration work is what we do, and it is exactly what protects you when a fixed-price vendor misses (their own fielded-fleet record shows why it matters)."
  • They say: "The enterprise agreement already covers integration and support; we don't need a services contract." We say: "An enterprise agreement covers the vendor's products on the vendor's terms. Ask who verifies performance claims, who integrates the systems they compete against, and who represents the government's technical position at the table. That cannot be the same company cashing the product check."
  • They say: "Their CCA test showed airmen operating it with just a laptop; test support footprints are shrinking." We say: "Lean operations at the flight line is real progress, and it shifts the work rather than deleting it: autonomy verification, test design, range integration, safety cases, and data analysis grow as autonomy scales. We should size the government test enterprise for that, not assume it away."
  • They say: "DoD policy favors buying commercial products over paying for development services." We say: "Agreed, and buying commercial well is a skill. Smart-buyer engineering, acceptance testing, and OCI-clean integration are how you get the benefits of commercial speed without inheriting the vendor's risk."

Trap-Setting Questions

  • "When the integrator also manufactures competing sensors and effectors, who independently verifies the trade studies and the performance claims?"
  • "Can you get government-verified fleet availability data for their currently fielded systems over the last 24 months, not demo results?"
  • "If every task order is firm-fixed-price and the vendor is running a multi-billion-dollar annual operating loss, what protects your program's support tail five years from now?"
  • "What is the exit plan and data-rights position if you need to swap the C2 layer or add a sensor the vendor competes with?"
  • "How will the government retain an independent test and acceptance capability as vendor-embedded teams take over more of the fielding pipeline?"

Pricing Posture

They do not compete on labor rates; they price products and enterprise subscriptions firm-fixed-price and use private capital to absorb early losses, a buy-in posture at a scale services firms cannot match (projected ~$1.2B operating loss for 2026 while revenue doubles) [S1][S5]. Analyst reporting describes a strategy of software-like margins on Lattice once hardware is emplaced [S1]. Implication for us: never fight them on price for productized scope; fight the scope definition, so integration, verification, and government-side engineering stay severable and services-shaped. No wrap-rate intel exists or applies. (needs internal input: any pricing behavior observed where they teamed with or against us on task orders)

Intel & Proof Points

PP1 — Army enterprise agreement, up to $20B over 10 years (Mar 2026)

The Army awarded Anduril a 10-year enterprise contract with a cumulative ceiling of $20B on 14 Mar 2026, consolidating 120+ separate procurement actions for its commercial solutions (Lattice suite, hardware, data, and support) into one framework; coverage notes all task orders are firm-fixed-price [S3][S4][S5].

PP2 — JIATF-401 names Lattice preferred counter-drone C2 ($87M, Mar 2026)

The first task order under the enterprise agreement: an $87M award establishing Lattice as the preferred tactical counter-UAS command-and-control software for the joint task force [S6].

PP3 — SOCOM counter-UAS systems integration partner IDIQ (2022, ceiling ~$1.9B)

SOCOM selected Anduril from 12 offerors in Jan 2022 as counter-UAS systems integration partner under a ~$967.6M 10-year IDIQ, chartering it to integrate third-party sensors and effectors alongside its own family of systems; the ceiling was nearly doubled to ~$1.9B in Nov 2024 [S7][S8].

PP4 — SOCOM mission autonomy SIP ($86M, Mar 2025)

SOCOM awarded Anduril an $86M contract as mission autonomy systems integration partner for autonomy software integration [S9].

PP5 — CCA production decision (Jun 2026)

The USAF ordered both Anduril's FQ-44 and General Atomics' FQ-42 into production in Jun 2026, with a combined 150 aircraft planned by 2030; the YFQ-44A first flew Oct 2025 and entered serial production at Arsenal-1 (Ohio) in Mar 2026 [S10][S11].

PP6 — Blue-suit CCA test operations at Edwards (Apr 2026)

The Experimental Operations Unit (53rd Wing, Eglin) ran multi-sortie YFQ-44A tests at Edwards AFB in Apr 2026 using front-line maintainers, not contractor crews, who launched, recovered, and turned the aircraft with a ruggedized laptop; the narrative directly supports smaller test/ops support footprints [S12][S13].

PP7 — IVAS novation and SBMC prototype award (2025)

The Army approved novation of Microsoft's IVAS contract to Anduril on 10 Apr 2025 (Anduril sustains existing devices rather than building more), and in Sep 2025 awarded Anduril a $159M SBMC prototype deal (Rivet got $195M); Anduril's EagleEye headset teams with Meta for optics/display [S14][S15][S29].

PP8 — Huntsville expansion with retired LTG Neil Thurgood (announced via company news)

Anduril hired retired Army LTG Neil Thurgood (formerly led Army RCCTO) as SVP and senior site executive in Huntsville, responsible for counter-UAS, air and missile defense, tactical weapons, mission systems, and C2 delivery; the company operates offices in Huntsville and recruits technical staff there [S16].

PP9 — Documented execution problems (Mar–Jun 2026)

WIRED's Mar 2026 investigation reported safety issues, production delays, management churn, a rocket-motor mass-production slip of more than a year, and at least four 2025 military tests in which Anduril systems underperformed; in late Jun 2026 a rocket motor exploded during a test at the Mississippi site, damaging the test stand [S18][S19].

PP10 — CBP tower fleet scale and readiness problems

Anduril's autonomous surveillance towers for CBP sit under an IDIQ with a $2B ceiling (~$818M obligated as of Dec 2025); EFF reporting citing 2024 Border Patrol memos said roughly 30% of towers were broken, with chronic desert-conditions problems [S20][S21].

PP11 — GAO protest history (B-419420)

Anduril protested an Air Force task order solicitation for tactical edge node support (FA8612-21-R-0601) as out of scope of the underlying IDIQs; GAO denied the protest and dismissed the restrictive-competition ground for lack of jurisdiction [S17].

PP12 — Financial profile (2025–2026)

Roughly $2.2B revenue in 2025 and a company-confirmed $4.3B projection for 2026; $5B Series H (May 2026) at a $61B valuation with ~$11.9B raised in total; projected ~$1.2B 2026 operating loss with adjusted-EBITDA profitability not expected until about 2030 [S1][S2][S30].

PP13 — Golden Dome C2 software role with Palantir (Mar 2026)

Reuters-sourced reporting says Anduril and Palantir are developing command-and-control software for the Golden Dome missile-defense initiative, processing distributed sensor data to coordinate weapons against aerial threats [S22].
