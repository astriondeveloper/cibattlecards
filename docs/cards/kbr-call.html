Threat Summary

KBR hurts us most as the entrenched, low-risk incumbent in NASA human spaceflight and as a resident engineering house at Redstone. On the NASA side it runs mission planning, astronaut and flight-controller training, and real-time flight execution for the ISS, Orion, SLS, and Artemis under IMOC III, and it just won the estimated $2.459B astronaut-health HHPC, so on any human-spaceflight-adjacent services bid at JSC or MSFC it carries directly relevant, current past performance and an embedded workforce [S8][S9]. At Redstone, KBR Wyle Services sits inside PEO Missiles and Space doing tactical-missile and Patriot systems engineering and a multi-weapon-system SETA scope through 2031, the same aviation-and-missile customer community we support [S11]. It reaches military-space and connected-battlespace work through LinQuest and Centauri, and it pre-qualifies on most large task orders through ASTRO (eight prime seats), OASIS+, and SeaPort-NxG [S12][S13][S17]. Where KBR seldom beats us is a focused, labor-driven T&E, SETA, or mission-engineering competition scored on dedicated key personnel and stable, senior program attention. And KBR is unusually beatable on continuity right now: it is spinning its entire government business into a new company through early 2027, it lost the $1.8B COSMOS NASA recompete it had effectively held since 2018 and failed two protests, and its government-segment revenue was essentially flat year over year while contingency work in Europe fell away [S4][S5][S6][S2][S1]. Those give us clean, scoreable discriminators on focus, stability, and transition risk.

Their Discriminators

  • NASA human-spaceflight incumbency and workforce. Decades of plan-train-fly and astronaut-health delivery mean the customer sees KBR as the people who train the astronauts and run the mission-control floor, not a vendor to be onboarded [S8][S9][S14].
  • Redstone and Huntsville engineering mass through KBR Wyle Services. Resident missile and aviation systems-engineering and SETA staff embedded with PEO Missiles and Space and the AMCOM community [S11].
  • Military-space and battlespace breadth from LinQuest and Centauri. Space Force, Air Force, and intelligence relationships, plus directed-energy and cyber, that KBR pulls into adjacent bids [S12][S13].
  • Turnkey mission-operations delivery. A single prime bundling engineering, operations, training, and logistics, attractive to a customer who wants one accountable prime for an end-to-end mission [S8].
  • Scale, vehicle access, and balance sheet. A ~$7.8B company with roughly $23B in backlog and options and prime seats on ASTRO, OASIS+, and SeaPort-NxG that let it carry big IDIQs at low perceived risk [S1][S2][S17].

Our Counter-Positioning

  • Sell focus and stability against W1, W2, and W5. Position steady, dedicated staffing and low transition risk against a competitor that is carving out its entire government business, standing up a new public company with new leadership and no announced brand, and losing incumbent recompetes even in NASA. Make "who will hold this program steady through their own corporate separation" a live evaluation question.
  • Attack the incumbency assumption against W2. Where KBR is the sitting incumbent, the COSMOS loss shows incumbency is not destiny. Press for continuity and transition-risk criteria, named key personnel, and evidence that the program survives the offeror's reorganization.
  • Force focused, labor-scored terms against W4. Where the buy is dominated by direct engineering labor and dedicated key personnel, insist on fully burdened rate realism and named on-contract staff so a large multinational wrap cannot hide behind scale.
  • Be honest about local presence. At Redstone, Huntsville, and JSC, KBR has genuine resident mass, so proximity is not our wedge there; our wedge is focus, dedicated senior attention, and stability. At the Air Force test enterprise (Edwards, Eglin) and other sites, confirm KBR's actual on-site footprint before asserting a local-bench gap, because the Wyle heritage gave them real AFMC test presence. (needs internal input: confirm KBR and KBR Wyle on-site headcount and active task orders at AFTC and at our anchor customers before claiming a specific staffing gap.)
  • Use OCI situationally, not as the lead. Unlike a hardware OEM, KBR builds few of the systems under test, so the independence lever is narrower here than against product primes; deploy it only where KBR operates, sustains, integrates, or trains on the very system it would also be asked to assess independently. (needs internal input: any teaming, NDA, or deconfliction history with KBR or its LinQuest and Centauri units.)

Objection Handling

  • They say: "KBR trains the astronauts and flies the missions, so nobody knows NASA human spaceflight better." We say: Deep familiarity is real, but NASA just moved its $1.8B mission-operations work to a different team and the decision was upheld on protest. Score dedicated key personnel, transition risk, and continuity for this actual scope, not incumbency on programs that are themselves changing hands.
  • They say: "KBR is a global company with the scale to carry this at low risk." We say: That global company is in the middle of splitting its government business into a brand-new public company. Ask every offeror how this program is insulated from their own reorganization, and what percentage of named key personnel are committed full time to it.
  • They say: "They already sit at Redstone with the missile and aviation engineers." We say: Resident staff is a starting point, not a delivery plan. Score the burdened rate, the named key personnel, and the dedicated senior attention this effort will receive, rather than corporate presence spread across missiles, chemicals, and contingency logistics.
  • They say: "A large incumbent is the safe choice." We say: Ask what has changed on their side. A full corporate spin-off, new leadership, flat government revenue, and a recent lost NASA recompete make continuity the real risk question. A stable, focused team is the lower-risk path on a labor-driven scope.

Trap-Setting Questions

  • "How will the evaluation ensure that a pending corporate separation, spin-off, or reorganization on an offeror's side will not disrupt staffing, senior management attention, or reach-back during the period of performance?"
  • "What percentage of proposed key personnel will be committed full time to this effort and resident at the government site at contract start, rather than matrixed from a broad, multi-market corporate portfolio?"
  • "How will offerors demonstrate continuity and transition-risk mitigation, given recent turnover of incumbents on comparable mission-operations and engineering recompetes?"
  • "Where an offeror operates, sustains, integrates, or trains on the systems relevant to this effort, how will it identify and mitigate organizational conflicts of interest so the government preserves independent engineering and assessment?"
  • "How will cited past performance be validated for relevance at the size and services scope of this effort, as opposed to much larger operations-and-logistics or platform-integration programs?"
