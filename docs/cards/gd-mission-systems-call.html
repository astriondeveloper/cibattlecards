Threat Summary

GDMS hurts us where an engineering-services scope sits next to hardware they already own. On MUOS ground sustainment they are the sole-source incumbent with up to $2.2B of runway [S12][S13][S14]; on SDA ground they hold both the operations-and-integration and ground-management franchises [S6][S7]; on submarines Progeny gives them the software and lifecycle-services lane inside NAVSEA programs [S21]. When a customer bundles integration services with the fielded baseline, we start from behind. The compounding threat is the GD family play: GDMS takes the product-adjacent work while GDIT primes the large consolidated services contracts (for example the $988M Navy SACSS C5ISR support award) [S15], squeezing mid-size primes from both sides. They rarely meet us head-on in SETA, T&E, or range support; the fight is on systems-integration and O&M task orders at SSC/SDA, NAVSEA, and Army C5ISR programs.

Their Discriminators

  • Ownership of fielded baselines: the customer's crypto devices, satcom ground system, or combat-system components are often GDMS products, which makes them the default choice for the surrounding engineering and sustainment work [S11][S12][S21].
  • NSA-certified crypto pedigree and classified infrastructure: Type 1 encryption franchise (NGLD-M: 265,000 units planned over 10 years) that few companies can touch [S11].
  • Proliferated-constellation ground expertise at SDA: they designed and operate the Tranche 1 ops centers and won the follow-on ground management and integration design contract [S7][S6].
  • Corporate reach-back and balance sheet of a $52.6B prime [S4], plus a services sibling (GDIT) that lets the GD family bid both product and services sides of a mission area [S3][S15].
  • Seven decades of strategic-deterrence weapon C2 heritage that anchors credibility with nuclear-enterprise customers [S27].

Our Counter-Positioning

  • Sell OEM independence against W1. We own no hardware baseline, so our engineering, integration support, and T&E advice carries no product agenda and no OCI mitigation plan the customer has to police. Push evaluation language on objectivity, data rights, and open-architecture verification wherever their products sit on the program.
  • Against their baseline incumbency (MUOS, SDA ground, submarine electronics), do not attack the product; attack the wrap. Position us for the government-side engineering, independent assessment, integration V&V, and test scopes that a prudent PMO keeps separate from the OEM.
  • Exploit W3 by forcing services evaluations onto services terms: dedicated key personnel, unit-level past performance relevance, and program-office intimacy. Their strongest services CPARS often belongs to a sibling unit, not the bidding entity.
  • Exploit W5 at Redstone, AFTC, and MDA: our people already sit with these customers. Make local bench, clearance density on site, and surge-in-place a scored discriminator.
  • On software-centric work (W4), stress our agile delivery record and commercial tooling; frame MIL-SPEC integration heritage as the wrong reference class for the scope.
  • Where they are genuinely strong (space ground, crypto, submarine combat systems), consider teaming or deconfliction rather than head-on primes; the winnable ground is the services adjacency, not the baseline.

Objection Handling

  • They say: "The OEM already knows the system; picking anyone else adds risk." We say: OEM familiarity is real, and it is exactly why the government separates independent engineering and test from the baseline owner. We give the PMO an advocate with no product revenue at stake, and we inherit system knowledge through government-furnished artifacts and data rights the customer already paid for.
  • They say: "They bring 40,000-person corporate reach-back." We say: Reach-back that is shared across an entire segment is a staffing promise, not a staffing plan. Ask every offeror what percent of named key personnel are dedicated full time to this contract and sitting locally. That is a question we win.
  • They say: "Their past performance is bigger." We say: Bigger is not more relevant. Their headline contracts are product and ground-system builds; ours are services of this scope, at this customer, at this contract size. CPARS relevance criteria, applied honestly, favor us.
  • They say: "They won the open-architecture prototype, so they own the roadmap." We say: A prototype OTA is not a production franchise, and open standards exist precisely so the government is never dependent on one vendor. We help the customer keep it that way.

Trap-Setting Questions

  • "How will you ensure offerors with fielded hardware or software baselines on this program identify and mitigate organizational conflicts of interest, so the government keeps independent engineering advice?"
  • "Should the evaluation require that cited past performance was executed by the specific business unit proposing, rather than an affiliated company?"
  • "What percentage of proposed key personnel will be dedicated full time and resident within commuting distance of the government site on day one?"
  • "How will offerors demonstrate that integration artifacts and interface documentation will be delivered with government purpose rights, so follow-on work stays competitive?"
  • "Can offerors show recent agile software delivery on programs of this type, as opposed to hardware-baseline integration heritage?"
