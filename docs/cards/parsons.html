Overview

Parsons Corporation (Chantilly, VA; NYSE: PSN; founded 1944) is a publicly traded defense, intelligence, and infrastructure engineering firm reporting $6.4B FY2025 revenue and about 19,000 employees, split roughly in half between a Federal Solutions segment (~$3.2B) and a Critical Infrastructure segment (~$3.14B) [S1][S2][S17]. We do not collide with the infrastructure half (transportation, water, environmental, and a large Middle East program-management business), so this card is about their Federal Solutions defense and national-security work [S12]. The collision is concentrated and real: Parsons is the Missile Defense Agency's systems-engineering-and-integration contractor through the seven-year, up-to-$2.24B TEAMS-Next award, and in December 2025 it won a seat on SHIELD, the 10-year, $151B-ceiling MDA IDIQ that succeeds TEAMS and underpins the Golden Dome architecture [S4][S5]. That work sits in our Huntsville backyard: Parsons opened a 27,000-sq-ft facility at Redstone Gateway (outside Gate 9) with a 21,000-sq-ft high-bay expressly to fast-track Golden Dome and missile-defense program support, next to the MDA and Army missile-defense community we support [S6]. Beyond missile defense they are building fast in space (a $185M Space Force situational-awareness contract), cyber (a $137M DTRA task order; a SealingTech Joint Cyber Hunt Kit production line USCYBERCOM values up to $500M), and electronic warfare and SIGINT, largely through a run of acquisitions (BlackSignal, Chesapeake/CTI, Altamira) [S7][S8][S9][S10]. Where we meet them is missile-defense engineering and integration, space services, and cyber/EW at Redstone and the Space Force; where we do not is their infrastructure segment, their intelligence-community accounts, and their international footprint. Our edge is a resident, badged test-and-evaluation and mission-engineering bench at the ranges and a focused business, against a $6.4B multi-market prime that grows by acquisition and just absorbed a several-hundred-million-dollar revenue shock from one confidential program [S1][S3].

Threat Summary

Parsons hurts us most in the missile-defense engineering-and-integration lane, where they are the sitting MDA systems-engineering incumbent (TEAMS-Next, up to $2.24B) and now hold a SHIELD seat on the $151B vehicle that will carry much of the Golden Dome work at Redstone [S4][S5]. Their new high-bay facility at Redstone Gateway signals intent to be physically resident in our Huntsville missile-defense community, not to compete from a distance [S6]. They are also arming quickly in the adjacent lanes we touch: space situational awareness for the Space Force, DTRA cyber operations, and an acquisition-fueled electronic-warfare, SIGINT, and cyber-kit portfolio that lets them wrap a full-spectrum national-security story around a bid [S7][S8][S9][S10]. Where they seldom beat us is a focused, labor-driven test-and-evaluation, mission-engineering, or SETA competition scored on resident on-site key personnel and independence, because Parsons' center of gravity is centralized SE&I, intelligence, and a very large infrastructure business, and because a single confidential State Department program swinging their revenue by hundreds of millions, a serial-acquisition integration load, and two recent recompete or eligibility stumbles all give us clean, scoreable discriminators [S3][S10][S13][S14].

Their Discriminators

  • MDA systems-engineering incumbency. Parsons holds the MDA systems-engineering-and-integration seat (TEAMS-Next, up to $2.24B) and a SHIELD position, which makes them the vendor-agnostic integrator the customer already trusts for the layered missile-defense architecture, a hard position to dislodge on any adjacent missile-defense scope [S4][S5].
  • Golden Dome alignment and Huntsville presence. Their existing MDA role plus a purpose-built Redstone Gateway high-bay facility position them as an early, resident Golden Dome player rather than a newcomer [S5][S6].
  • Full-spectrum national-security breadth. Cyber (offensive and defensive), electronic warfare, SIGINT, and space, assembled through acquisitions, let them offer one accountable prime across the mission set on a large program [S8][S9][S10].
  • Scale and balance sheet. A $6.4B, ~19,000-person public prime with record adjusted EBITDA and an active M&A engine (2-4 deals a year) can carry large, high-ceiling programs at low perceived risk and buy its way into new capability quickly [S1][S16].
  • Space software and PNT franchises. A government-owned situational-awareness software suite and assured-PNT heritage give them installed positions the Space Force customer already runs [S7].

Strengths

  • MDA SE&I incumbency: the seven-year, up-to-$2.24B TEAMS-Next award makes Parsons MDA's systems-engineering-and-integration contractor, delivering vendor-agnostic engineering analysis and modeling and simulation for an integrated, layered missile-defense system [S4].
  • SHIELD / Golden Dome position: a 10-year seat on the $151B MDA SHIELD IDIQ, covering roughly 19 technical and engineering activities supporting the Golden Dome architecture, positions them for the highest-growth missile-defense spend of the decade [S5].
  • Resident Huntsville footprint: a new 27,000-sq-ft Redstone Gateway facility (21,000 sq ft of high-bay) built to fast-track Golden Dome development, test, and deployment work next to MDA [S6].
  • Space services: a $185M five-year Space Force contract expanding a government-owned integrated situational-awareness software suite, plus space domain awareness, space C2, and assured-PNT capability [S7].
  • Cyber and EW depth from acquisitions: DTRA cyber operations ($137M AEMSS task order), a SealingTech Joint Cyber Hunt Kit production line USCYBERCOM values up to $500M, plus BlackSignal (EW, counter-space RF), CTI (electromagnetic warfare, SOF/INDOPACOM), and Altamira (SIGINT, missile warning, space) [S8][S9][S10].
  • Financial scale and momentum: $6.4B FY2025 revenue, record adjusted EBITDA of $609M at a 9.6% margin, net income $241M, 100% free-cash-flow conversion, and an M&A engine targeting 2-4 deals in 2026 [S1][S16].
  • Contract-vehicle access: positions on ASTRO and Alliant 2, plus agency IDIQs including TEAMS-Next, SHIELD, and DTRA AEMSS [S19].
  • Diversified base: a ~$3.14B Critical Infrastructure segment (+14.6% in FY2025) and a $1B-plus Middle East business give revenue that is not dependent on any single defense budget line [S2][S12].

Weaknesses

W1 - Revenue concentration and volatility from one confidential program

A single confidential State Department program (program management and technology services, 10-year, then in year three) swung Parsons' revenue by hundreds of millions when the 2025 State reorganization eliminated the program office: the contract ran at ~80% of volume in Q1 2025, ~50% in Q2, and was essentially zeroed for the rest of the year, driving Federal Solutions down 20% to ~$3.2B and carrying a ~$345M revenue headwind into 2026 [S3][S2][S16]. That fragility is the opposite of the steady, dedicated engineering base a customer wants on a mission-critical recompete, and it is documented in their own guidance revisions.

W2 - Serial-acquisition roll-up: integration, culture, and debt load

Much of Parsons' Federal capability is bolted on, not grown: nine of eleven strategic acquisitions since 2017 sit in Federal Solutions, and 2023-2026 alone brought SealingTech, BlackSignal ($203.8M), CTI ($89M), and Altamira (up to $375M), with management targeting 2-4 more deals in 2026 [S10][S11][S16]. A roll-up at that pace carries integration risk, cultural churn, retention risk on acquired key personnel, and goodwill and leverage on the balance sheet. On a focused engineering recompete a customer can reasonably ask whether the team executing is a stable, integrated organization or a recently assembled set of logos still being merged.

W3 - Half the company, and much management attention, is infrastructure and international

About half of Parsons' revenue is the Critical Infrastructure segment (~$3.14B), and a $1B-plus slice of that is Middle East transportation and giga-project work (NEOM, the ~$7B King Salman International Airport) that is not the defense-engineering-services fight we are in [S12][S2]. On a focused missile-defense, test-and-evaluation, or mission-engineering competition, a dedicated engineering company can credibly argue that the customer's mission is one line inside a diversified, half-civil, heavily international portfolio, while for us it is the core of the business.

W4 - Centralized SE&I model, not a resident test-and-evaluation and SETA bench at the ranges

Parsons' defense center of gravity is centralized systems-engineering-and-integration, intelligence, and space software, delivered largely as an MDA integrator and through acquired product lines, rather than a badged, on-site test-and-evaluation and SETA workforce embedded at the Air Force test centers and Army ranges where our people sit [S4][S7]. Their verified new physical presence is a Redstone Gateway program-support facility oriented to Golden Dome, not a distributed range-and-test-enterprise bench at Eglin, Edwards, or the Army test community [S6]. That geography is a scoreable gap wherever local, on-site engineering and T&E presence is valued. (needs internal input: confirm Parsons' actual on-site headcount and active task orders at AFTC (Eglin, Edwards), at Redstone, and across the Army test enterprise before asserting a specific staffing gap in a proposal; note their Redstone Gateway facility gives them genuine, growing local presence for missile-defense program support, so this argument is strongest at the USAF test centers and in staffed T&E, not blanket at Huntsville.)

W5 - Recent recompete and eligibility stumbles

Parsons does not always win, and has stumbled on the mechanics: GAO denied its protest after it lost a GSA Alliant 2 cyber task order to ManTech, which the agency found technically superior (decision posted November 2024), and GAO dismissed the Amentum-Parsons LOGCAP V protest for lack of standing after finding the joint venture's proposal internally inconsistent on a material requirement and therefore ineligible for award (September 2025) [S13][S14]. Capture teams can frame proposal discipline, technical relevance, and eligibility as live, scoreable risks a large incumbent cannot simply assume away.

W6 - Prime-scale overhead on labor-driven competitions

Parsons is a $6.4B prime carrying corporate overhead, indirect structure, and shareholder margin expectations (record FY2025 adjusted EBITDA margin of 9.6%) [S1]. On engineering-services, SETA, and test-and-evaluation competitions where evaluated price turns on the fully burdened labor rate rather than on program scale or a proprietary kit, a large multi-market prime's wrap is beatable by a focused mid-size firm. This is team judgment, not documented rate intelligence. (needs internal input: any observed evaluated-price or wrap-rate data from shared task-order competitions at MDA, the Space Force, or the Army/USAF test enterprise.)

Our Counter-Positioning

  • Win the resident-bench argument against W4. Our people are badged and resident at the Air Force test enterprise (AFTC: Eglin, Edwards) and across the Redstone Army missile-and-aviation community today. Make staffed local presence, on-site clearance density, and surge-in-place a scored factor where it applies, and be honest internally that at Redstone their new facility gives them real, growing proximity, so the sharpest wedge there is focus and dedicated T&E, not simple geography.
  • Sell continuity and stability against W1 and W2. On any recompete, position steady, dedicated, integrated staffing against a prime whose revenue just took a several-hundred-million-dollar hit from one program and whose Federal capability is a fast-moving roll-up of recently acquired companies still being merged.
  • Force focused, labor-scored terms against W3 and W6. Where the buy is dominated by direct engineering labor and dedicated key personnel, insist on burdened-rate realism, named on-contract staff, and evidence that the customer's mission gets dedicated senior attention rather than a matrixed slice of a half-civil, heavily international $6.4B portfolio.
  • Respect the MDA incumbency, then compete around it. Their TEAMS/SHIELD SE&I seat is a genuine strength; do not bid head-on into their integrator role. Compete for the discrete engineering, test, mission-engineering, and analysis scopes where dedicated key personnel and independence outscore integrator incumbency, and consider teaming or deconfliction where they hold the SE&I baseline. (needs internal input: any existing teaming, NDA, or deconfliction history with Parsons on MDA or Space Force work.)
  • Use their proposal stumbles against W5. On competitions where they are favored, plant relevance-of-past-performance and eligibility discipline as evaluation themes, and make sure our own compliance is airtight so the mechanics cut our way.

Objection Handling

  • They say: "Parsons already runs MDA systems engineering, so they know the missile-defense architecture." We say: Systems-engineering-and-integration incumbency is exactly why the government wants independent, dedicated engineering and test talent around it. Score this effort on the named key personnel who will actually do the work and their resident presence, not on a corporate integrator relationship on a different contract.
  • They say: "They bring scale and a full national-security portfolio a mid-size firm can't match." We say: Scale spread across a half-civil, heavily international $6.4B portfolio is a staffing promise, not a staffing plan. Ask every offeror what percentage of named key personnel are dedicated full time to this mission and resident at your site.
  • They say: "Parsons has been buying the best cyber, EW, and space capabilities." We say: Acquired capability is only as good as its integration and retention. Ask offerors to show the team on this effort is a stable, integrated organization with committed key personnel, not a recently merged set of companies still finding its footing.
  • They say: "A large public prime is the safe, low-risk choice." We say: Ask what has changed on their side. A single confidential program just swung their revenue by hundreds of millions, and recent recompete and eligibility rulings show incumbency and award are not automatic. A focused team with stable, dedicated staff is the lower-risk path on a labor-driven scope.

Trap-Setting Questions

  • "What percentage of proposed key personnel will be dedicated full time and resident within commuting distance of the government site at contract start, rather than matrixed from a broad, multi-market corporate portfolio?"
  • "How will the evaluation confirm that an offeror's cited experience was performed by the team and personnel proposed here, rather than inherited from recently acquired companies still being integrated?"
  • "How will offerors demonstrate continuity and financial stability of the delivering organization across the full period of performance, including resilience to revenue swings on unrelated large programs?"
  • "How will past performance be checked for relevance at the size and services scope of this actual effort, as opposed to systems-engineering-integration or infrastructure heritage on much larger or unrelated programs?"
  • "How will offerors demonstrate independent, objective engineering and test judgment where the same firm also supplies proprietary hardware, kits, or integration baselines in the mission area?"

Pricing Posture

Facts: Parsons is a ~$6.4B prime that posted a record FY2025 adjusted EBITDA margin of 9.6% and 100% free-cash-flow conversion, roughly half of company revenue coming from a Critical Infrastructure segment that carries different margin dynamics than labor-driven defense engineering [S1][S2]. Judgment, labeled as such: a multi-market prime of this size typically wraps higher than a focused mid-size services firm on labor-rate-driven test, SETA, and mission-engineering competitions, so they are most beatable where evaluated price turns on the burdened rate rather than on program scale or a proprietary product/kit. Expect them to price most aggressively where scale, an installed SE&I or software franchise, or a growth priority (Golden Dome, space, cyber kits) justifies leaning in, and to protect margin on labor-only recompetes. We hold no documented rate intelligence on their engineering-services entities. Intel & Proof Points

PP1 - Scale, segments, and workforce

Parsons reported FY2025 (year ended 2025-12-31) revenue of $6.4B, record adjusted EBITDA of $609M at a 9.6% margin, net income of $241M, and diluted EPS of $2.20, with 100% free-cash-flow conversion; the company employs about 19,000 people and is headquartered in Chantilly, VA [S1][S17].

PP2 - MDA TEAMS-Next systems engineering

The Missile Defense Agency awarded Parsons the TEAMS-Next Systems Engineering contract, a seven-year vehicle valued up to $2,241,762,696 (contract HQ085821C0015), under which Parsons serves as MDA's systems-engineering-and-integration contractor, delivering vendor-agnostic engineering analysis and modeling and simulation for an integrated, layered missile-defense system [S4].

PP3 - MDA SHIELD / Golden Dome

On 2025-12-22 Parsons won a position on the MDA SHIELD (Scalable Homeland Innovative Enterprise Layered Defense) IDIQ, a 10-year vehicle with a $151B ceiling covering roughly 19 technical and engineering activities supporting the Golden Dome missile-defense architecture; SHIELD succeeds Parsons' TEAMS work [S5].

PP4 - Redstone Gateway facility

Parsons opened a new 27,000-sq-ft facility at Redstone Gateway just outside Redstone Arsenal's Gate 9 in Huntsville, AL, including 21,000 sq ft of high-bay space, expressly to fast-track development, testing, and deployment of Golden Dome and other national-security efforts near the MDA community [S6].

PP5 - Confidential-program revenue shock

A confidential State Department program (program management and technology services, in year three of ten) ran at ~80% of volume in Q1 2025, ~50% in Q2, and was essentially zeroed for the rest of 2025 after the State reorganization eliminated the responsible program office, driving Federal Solutions revenue down 20% to ~$3.2B and carrying a ~$345M revenue headwind into 2026 [S3][S2][S16].

PP6 - Space Force situational awareness

Parsons won a $185M, five-year Space Force contract for IT and engineering services expanding a government-owned integrated situational-awareness software suite, part of a space portfolio spanning space domain awareness, space C2, and assured PNT [S7].

PP7 - DTRA cyber operations

Parsons received a $137M DTRA cyber-operations task order (June 2025) under the AEMSS IDIQ MATOC, its third task order under that vehicle, covering cyber assessments and subject-matter experts for cyber operations, development, analysis, and research [S8].

PP8 - SealingTech Joint Cyber Hunt Kit

SealingTech, a Parsons company, received a USCYBERCOM intent to award a sole-source production contract for its Joint Cyber Hunt Kit, with an anticipated ceiling up to $500M; JCHK moving to production is a stated driver of Parsons' second-half 2026 ramp [S9].

PP9 - Acquisition roll-up in defense and intelligence

Parsons has assembled much of its Federal capability by acquisition: BlackSignal Technologies (2024, $203.8M; EW, counter-space RF, cyber), Chesapeake Technology International (2025, $89M, ~225 employees; electromagnetic warfare, SOF/INDOPACOM), and Altamira Technologies (announced 2026-01-15, up to $375M; 600+ employees, 90%+ cleared; SIGINT, missile warning, space), on top of SealingTech (2023, $179.3M); nine of eleven strategic acquisitions since 2017 sit in Federal Solutions, with 2-4 more targeted in 2026 [S10][S11].

PP10 - Half the company is infrastructure and international

Parsons' Critical Infrastructure segment posted ~$3.14B in FY2025 revenue (+14.6%); its Middle East business alone carried more than $1B in annual revenue in 2024 with ~7,000 GCC employees, including NEOM and a JV role on the ~$7B King Salman International Airport program in Riyadh, none of it in the defense-engineering-services fight [S12][S2].

PP11 - Alliant 2 cyber loss and denied protest

GAO denied Parsons' protest (decision posted 2024-11-21, B-422849) after it lost a GSA Alliant 2 cyber task order to ManTech, which the agency found technically superior; GAO rejected Parsons' claim that ManTech gained an unfair advantage by hiring a former Navy principal cyber adviser [S13].

PP12 - LOGCAP V joint-venture ineligibility

GAO dismissed the Amentum-Parsons LOGCAP V protest for lack of standing (2025-09-18, B-422697.14), finding the joint venture's proposal internally inconsistent on a material requirement and therefore ineligible for award [S14].

PP13 - FY2026 guidance and drivers

FY2026 guidance assumes ~6.6% organic growth in Federal Solutions and ~6.1% in Critical Infrastructure, absorbs the ~$345M confidential-contract headwind, and depends on a second-half ramp from JCHK production and Riyadh infrastructure; Q1 2026 revenue was $1.5B (down 8% organic, up 8% excluding the confidential contract) [S16].

PP14 - Leadership

Carey A. Smith is Chair (since 2022-01-18), President, and CEO (since 2021-07-01); Matt Ofilos is CFO (since 2022-07-25, formerly AWS Worldwide Public Sector and Raytheon); the board added Ellen Lord (former USD for Acquisition & Sustainment) in 2022; the company moved its headquarters to Chantilly, VA in 2023 [S15].

PP15 - Contract-vehicle access

Parsons holds a position on ASTRO (multiple-award IDIQ, 10-year period beginning 2021-11-15) and Alliant 2 (GWAC, under Parsons Government Services Inc.), plus agency IDIQs including MDA TEAMS-Next, MDA SHIELD, and DTRA AEMSS [S19].
