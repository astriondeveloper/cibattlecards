Threat Summary

Our most frequent Tier 1 opponent at AFTC. They win on incumbency, customer intimacy, and scale; they lose when evaluations reward technical innovation, key-personnel stability, or price realism. Expect them to prime, to protest close losses, and to recruit our staff during transitions.

Their Discriminators

  • Twenty-year continuous presence at the primary AFTC ranges; evaluators know their program managers by name. [S1]
  • Self-performed instrumentation depot with rapid calibration turnaround. [S2]
  • Scale: can surge 100+ cleared staff from adjacent contracts. [S2]

Our Counter-Positioning

  • Against their incumbency (and W3): position our transition plan around named key personnel with signed retention agreements; make leadership stability an evaluation issue.
  • Against their scale (and W1): lead with our certified-instrumentation bench and offer named individuals, not labor categories.
  • Against their pricing (W2): anchor cost realism to incumbent actuals and make out-year escalation visible to evaluators.

Objection Handling

  • They say: "Nobody knows this range like the current team." We say: "The data says continuity is about people, not logos — we've committed the named engineers who will be on the range day one, with retention agreements, and our transition plan was rated low-risk on two comparable takeovers."
  • They say: "A transition this size is too risky mid-test-program." We say: "We've executed three incumbent-displacement transitions of this size in the last five years with zero missed test events — here's the reference contact for each."

Trap-Setting Questions

  • "How would you want an offeror to demonstrate retention of certified instrumentation staff across the whole period of performance?"
  • "Would it help the evaluation to see proposed rates mapped against current contract actuals?"
  • "How much weight will leadership continuity during transition carry?"
